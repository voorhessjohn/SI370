Problem 5¶

A smoothie equipment vendor comes in and says the can improve your (z-normalized) score by .06 points. Essentially, what the company is telling you is that if your score distribution was normal (as you calculated above) with a mean of 1 and stdev of .2 (both made up). You'll now be at a mean of 1.06 (and still have a stdev of .2)

The equipment is very expensive, so you're going to need to hire more judges to sample batches of the old and new. The difference should very clear and very significant (i.e., p < .01). You want to be really confident that the judges will catch the difference (a power of 90% or .9).

Just to get you started, let's say the original process gave us a mean score of 0, and stdev of .1. The new process should be at a mean of .06 and stdev of .1. If we generated only 6 samples of each, the p-value will be usually be pretty high (not significant). You can change the value of n in the cell below. Run it multiple times to see if you get a significant p-value.

Do a power calculation (by simulation) to find the number of samples you'd need to have a power of 90% of rejecting the null hypothesis at .01 (on this .06 improvement). (15 points)¶

In [31]:
nreps = 10000   # number of experiments
n = 20 # number of samples to start
power = 0 # starting point for power

while power < 0.9:  # stop if we hit .9
    origProcess = np.random.normal(0, .1, (n, nreps))
    newProcess = np.random.normal(.06, .1, (n, nreps))
    t, p = stats.ttest_ind(origProcess, newProcess) 
    power = (p < 0.01).sum()/nreps    
    print(n, power)  # print the sample size and power achieved
    n += 1  # add one more to the samples
